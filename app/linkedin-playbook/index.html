LinkedIn Playbook

Methodology reference + tracking. Not automation — intelligence for how to show up on LinkedIn as a seller worth responding to.

The Golden Rule
Engage with their content for 1-2 weeks BEFORE you ever send a connection request or DM. Like, comment, share. Make your name familiar. When you eventually message them, you're not a stranger — you're "that person who always has smart comments on my posts."
Connection Request Templates (300 char max)
After engaging with their content Hi [Name], I've been following your posts on [topic] — your take on [specific point] really resonated. Would love to connect and keep the conversation going.
Mutual connection Hi [Name], [Mutual] and I were discussing [topic] and your name came up as someone with real expertise here. Would love to connect.
Signal-driven (after Signal Console trigger) Hi [Name], noticed [Company] is [signal — hiring, expanding, etc.]. I work in this space and thought there might be some relevant overlap. Happy to connect.
Industry peer Hi [Name], I'm in the [industry/category] space and your background at [Company] caught my eye. Always good to connect with people doing interesting work in this area.
What Makes a Good vs. Bad Request
DO Reference something specific — their content, a mutual connection, a shared interest.
DO Keep it under 200 characters if possible. Shorter requests have higher acceptance rates.
DO Sound like a human, not a template. One genuine sentence beats three polished ones.
DON'T Pitch in the connection request. "I help companies like yours..." is a vendor flag. Instant decline.
DON'T Use "I'd love to pick your brain" — it's extractive. You're asking for their expertise without offering anything.
DON'T Send blank requests. No note = "I'm mass-connecting and you don't matter enough for a sentence."
The 3-Message Pattern
Rapport → Give-First → Ask. Never skip a step. Never send all three in the same week.
Message 1: Rapport (24-48 hours after acceptance)
Thanks for connecting, [Name]. Your post about [topic] was one of the better takes I've seen on that — especially [specific point]. Looking forward to following your work.
TIMING Wait 24-48 hours after they accept. Immediate DM feels transactional.
Message 2: Give-First (Day 3-5)
[Name] — saw this and thought of your work at [Company]: [link to article/benchmark/insight]. Specifically relevant to [what they care about]. No ask — just thought it was useful.
KEY The give must be genuinely valuable. Not your blog post. Not your product page. An external resource that helps them regardless of whether they buy from you.
Message 3: The Ask (Day 7-10)
[Name], based on what you've shared about [topic/challenge] — I think there's a conversation worth having. We've helped companies like [comparable] with [specific result] and the pattern matches what [their company] seems to be navigating. Would 15 minutes work to see if it's relevant? Happy to do [specific day] or [specific day].
KEY By now you've built 2 touchpoints of rapport. This ask feels earned, not cold. The specific day choice forces scheduling, not an interest decision.
DM Cadence Rules
Day 0
Connection accepted. Do NOT message immediately.
Day 1-2
Message 1: Rapport. Comment on their content first.
Day 3-5
Message 2: Give-first. Share an external resource. Zero ask.
Day 7-10
Message 3: The ask. Specific meeting request with day options.
Day 14+
If no response: one final touch. "No worries if the timing isn't right. I'll keep sharing relevant stuff as I come across it."
Content Engagement as Outbound
Your comments on their posts are outbound touches. Every thoughtful comment puts your name in front of them — and their network — without the stigma of a cold message.
How to Comment (Not Like a Bot)
DO Add a perspective they didn't mention. "Great point about X. I'd add that Y is becoming equally important because Z." This positions you as a peer, not a cheerleader.
DO Ask a genuine question. "Curious how this plays out for [specific segment]? We've seen [different pattern] in [your space]." Shows expertise while creating dialogue.
DO Share a relevant data point. "This matches what [research firm] found — [stat]. The trend is accelerating." Adds value to their post.
DON'T "Great post!" / "Love this!" / "🔥🔥🔥" — empty validation from a stranger is invisible. Worse: it's what bots do.
DON'T Pitch in comments. "We actually solve this at [Company]!" is the fastest way to get muted.
DON'T Comment on every post. 2-3 comments per week per prospect is the ceiling. More than that and you're a stalker.
When to Share Their Content
Share their post to your feed with a 2-3 sentence take that adds your perspective. Tag them. This does three things: puts your name in their notifications, exposes their content to your network (they appreciate this), and positions you as someone in their orbit — not a cold stranger.
BEST FOR C-Suite and VP personas who actively post. They notice who amplifies their content. When you eventually DM or email them, you're already a known name.
Your Own Content as Outbound
Post 1-2× per week about topics relevant to your buyer's world. Not product announcements. Not "we're hiring." Industry insights, benchmark data, provocative takes on common problems. When prospects visit your profile after receiving your outreach, your content should confirm: "This person knows my world."
FORMAT Short posts (under 200 words) with a hook in the first line outperform long-form. Start with a number, a contrarian take, or a question.
When InMail Is Appropriate
InMail has lower response rates than connection requests because it feels like an ad. Use sparingly. The only scenarios where InMail is the right channel:
1. They don't accept connections — some executives have closed networks. InMail is the only path.
2. Time-sensitive trigger — a leadership change or funding event where waiting 1-2 weeks for a connection accept would miss the window.
3. You have something ultra-specific — not a generic pitch but a reference to something only they would care about.
InMail Templates
Signal-driven (C-Suite) [Name] — I noticed [signal] at [Company]. For companies navigating this, the pattern we've seen is [1-sentence insight]. Not pitching — just thought the data point was relevant to your current priorities. If it is, happy to share more context. — [Your Name]
Mutual connection reference [Name], [Mutual Connection] mentioned you're the person to talk to about [topic] at [Company]. We've been working with companies in your space on [specific challenge] and the results have been surprising. Worth 10 minutes? I'll make it count. — [Your Name]
InMail Rules
DO Keep it under 100 words. InMails are read on mobile.
DO Make the first sentence hyper-specific to them. Generic first lines get deleted.
DON'T Use InMail for follow-ups. If they didn't respond to your InMail, they won't respond to another one.
DON'T Use InMail when a connection request would work. InMails cost credits. Connection requests are free.
