The Wayfinder.

One persistent horizontal element at the top of the workspace. Where you were, where you are, where the work is carrying you. Click to see the system's reasoning. Override or commit. No left rail, no auto-landing, no hallway.

Three states below — resting, expanded (inline disclosure), and Smart Ctrl+K open. Same workspace, same surface, three moments. The Wayfinder is always visible but never opens a room without you.

Doctrine this mockup is testing

The hallway is gone.

Canon Part III §6 names the hallway problem — too many equal-weight doors, the operator deciding "where do I go now." A left rail is a hallway with the doors drawn on it. The Wayfinder replaces both nav and triage with one ranked read of where the work is, always visible, never insistent.

No room is a destination by default. The 22 rooms are canonical, but they're summoned through Ctrl+K, not browsed.

Agency stays with the operator.

Charter §2.1 says the system has agency over itself; the operator has agency over their work. The Wayfinder shows a read, never opens a room without an explicit commit. The resting state is genuinely neutral — the workspace does not auto-land on a ranked queue.

Click-deeper is an explicit gesture, not hover, not auto-open. The system has to be asked to defend its pick. When asked, it shows everything — reasoning, sharpenings, prerequisites, and what else is pulsing.

Verbs first. Search fallback.

Smart Ctrl+K is a "what next?" interrogator, not a nav menu. The top three rows are contextual reads expressed as verbs the operator can commit to. Search-for-rooms-and-objects sits below as the fallback for when the operator already knows where they're going.

Verbs are the carrier of intent. Rooms are the place the verb lands. Object-first, command-first — canon Part I §3 — read backwards through the keyboard.
