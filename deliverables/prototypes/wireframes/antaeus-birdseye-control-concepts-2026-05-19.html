Antaeus · Birdseye control system · concept mockups · 2026-05-19

An always-present control surface that doesn't revive the hallway.

The reference apps (Apollo, Figma, Salesforce, PowerBI, Linear, Notion) all build the same shell: left-rail module list, top bar, contextual right rail. Canon forbids that — a left rail of 20 rooms would dissolve what Antaeus argues. These four concepts hold the always-present feeling while keeping the shape pressure-shaped, not destination-shaped.

Concept A

The Pressure Deck

A thin always-on strip across the top of every room. Three regions, left to right: focus chip (the object you're operating on right now), workspace verdict (readiness band + section-readiness), pressure dots (4 highest-pressure objects elsewhere — color-coded by family, hover for context, click to jump).

Tradeoff: very canon-aligned (object-first, ranked, no destinations) but adds vertical chrome to every room.

Concept B

The Pressure Compass

A vertical sliver running the full right edge with 4–6 dots stacked top-to-bottom — each is a top-pressure object across families (top deal, top signal, top proof, top ask, top recovery). Spatial logic: vertical position = pressure rank, color = family. Hover expands a tooltip with current state. Click jumps to that object in its native room.

Tradeoff: zero competition with the work surface, very distinctive — but customers expecting a left rail may need a moment to find it.

Concept C

Verdict Anchor + Recovery Bar

Tradeoff: most directly "control system feeling" — it literally looks like a topbar + statusbar — but adds two chrome strips per room.

Concept D

The Ambient HUD

A single 44px corner indicator (top-right) that pulses with workspace state — calm when nothing needs attention, blue with a count when something material changed, orange and pulsing when intervention is needed. Click → palette opens pre-filtered to whatever caused the pulse.

Tradeoff: lightest footprint, most canon-pure ("summoned" not central) — but lowest control-system feeling. Closest to "trust me, you don't need a nav."

Recommended

A + B layered, with C's recovery bar reserved for breach events.

The Pressure Deck (A) gives Sarah the constant orientation customers expect — "where am I, what's hot, what's the system doing." The Pressure Compass (B) on the right edge gives her always-visible cross-room state without enumerating rooms. C's recovery bar appears only on pressure breach — overdue, leaked, decayed — so it never reads as ambient chrome. D's HUD is folded into A's verdict pill (the pill pulses when D would have fired).

What this beats in the reference apps: Apollo shows you 20 modules and asks you to pick. Antaeus shows you the 5 objects under most pressure and lets you act. That's what Sarah notices on day one.
