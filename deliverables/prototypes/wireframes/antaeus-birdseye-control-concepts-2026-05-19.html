Antaeus · birdseye chrome ideation · 2026-05-19 (rewritten in the new voice the same day)

Show Sarah where the work is, without giving her a hallway.

Apollo, Figma, Salesforce, PowerBI, Linear, Notion all build the same shell: left-rail list of every module, a topbar, a contextual right rail. We can't do that — a left rail of 20 rooms would dissolve the thing that makes Antaeus useful (it tells you what to do first; the others list everything you could do). These four mockups hold the always-present feeling without enumerating rooms.

Concept A

A thin always-on strip across the top of every room.

Three pieces, left to right. Left: a chip naming the object Sarah is currently working on (account name + a phrase about its state). Center: a pill naming where the workspace stands overall (the Readiness level + section-ready count). Right: four small color-coded dots, one per highest-pressure object elsewhere in the workspace. Hover for context; click to jump.

What this costs and gives: very canon-aligned (object-first, ranked, no destinations to browse) but adds vertical chrome to every room.

Concept B

A thin always-on sliver running down the right edge.

Four to six small dots stacked top to bottom. Each dot is one of the highest-pressure objects across the workspace (top deal, top signal, top proof, top backchannel ask, top recovery). Vertical position shows rank (top = most pressure). Color shows which family it belongs to. Hover for the full context; click to jump to the room that owns it.

What this costs and gives: zero competition with the work surface, very distinctive — but a customer expecting a left rail on first open will need a moment to find this.

Concept D

A single small indicator in the top-right corner.

A 44px circle. Calm when nothing needs Sarah's attention. Blue with a count when something material changed somewhere. Orange and pulsing when something needs intervention. Click it; the palette opens already filtered to whatever caused the pulse.

Recommended

Layer A and B together. Hold C's bottom strip in reserve for when something goes wrong.

The top strip from A gives Sarah the constant orientation customers expect — what she's on, where the workspace stands, what else is hot. The right-edge sliver from B gives her always-visible cross-room pressure without enumerating rooms. C's bottom strip stays empty until something needs her to recover, so it never reads as ambient chrome. D's corner indicator folds into A's workspace pill (the pill pulses when D would have fired).

What Sarah notices on day one: Apollo gave her 20 modules and asked her to pick one. Antaeus gives her the 5 things under most pressure and lets her act.
