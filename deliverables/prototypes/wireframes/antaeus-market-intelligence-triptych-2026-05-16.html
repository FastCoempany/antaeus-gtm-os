Room concept · first-time-visitor landing · wired to onboarding industry

The system already has a read on your market. Before you log a thing, it should hand it to you.

The market intelligence room is the first thing a new operator sees after onboarding. It is not a dashboard. Not a feed. Not a "welcome, here are some tiles." It is an authored read on the industry the operator just named — the players, the pressures, the windows, the wedge — and the next move is to carry that read into the ICP, the territory, the signal radar. Three variants follow. Same mind. Different compositional postures.

Variant 01 · The Read

A market memo, authored by the system, with the operator's wedge as its conclusion.

Posture: the system speaks first. A sentence-shaped thesis at the top — long, severe, opinionated — sized like a Stratechery cover line. Below: three numbered paragraphs of synthesis (state of the field / who's winning / where the wedge is), each closing with an operator implication. The footer poses the room's question directly: what does this mean for your wedge?

Dominant move: Make your wedge → ICP Studio. Side routes recede.

Why it works: it earns the user's belief that this system has a read. Prose carries conviction in a way metric tiles never can.

The state of the field.

Three platforms — Harvey, Clearbrief, Spellbook — captured 71% of net-new logos in the last four quarters by selling into procurement, not legal. The GC pitch lost; the bundled-spend pitch won.

Every other vendor in this category is still building decks for the GC and being told "we'll bring it back to procurement." That's the kill.

Operator implication
If your wedge is "AI-native + GC champion," you're entering a closed sale. Pivot the wedge to a procurement-shaped problem before the first call.
02

Who is actually winning.

The winners are not the most technically advanced. They are the ones who arrive with a bundled-spend story — "we displace these three line-items at once" — and an MSA template procurement has already pre-vetted.

The losers arrive with a feature list, a free trial, and a champion who can't pull a budget.

Operator implication
Your first sale is the MSA, not the demo. Build the procurement-vetted contract before you build the pitch deck.
03

Where the wedge is open.

Two windows the three platforms are not defending: mid-market in-house teams (200–600 employees) who can't justify enterprise pricing, and specialist verticals (employment, IP, regulatory) where horizontal AI underperforms.

Both windows have a 4–6 quarter head start before the platforms turn their feature roadmap toward them.

Operator implication
Pick one window and define your ICP around it. Don't try to fight horizontally — you lose the bundled-spend story you don't have.

Variant 03 · The Operator's Read

Market intelligence as objects — Accounts, Signals, Motion windows — each one already routable into the system that matters.

Posture: command-first split spine. Left rail ranks every market object the system has already classified — specific named accounts, specific signals firing in the industry, specific motion windows still open — by relevance to the operator's just-named industry. Right rail is "what this means for your motion" on the selected object, with the per-object handoff into the right downstream room (Signal Console / Territory / ICP).

Dominant move is per row: Carry this into <the right room>. The market becomes compounding inventory the operator inherits, not a snapshot they admire.

Why it works: it makes the un-dashboard object-shaped like the rest of Antaeus. The user's first act is to grab a real piece of inheritable inventory — sacred-noun discipline preserved from minute one.
