# Sarah Chen — the navigation-audit persona

**Authority:** Locked 2026-05-17. The unit of measurement for every Phase 2 audit PR.

This document is what we test against. When a Phase 2 PR description says *"Sarah walks the {flow}"*, the Sarah referenced is the one in this file — not a generic operator, not a composite, not whichever CRO we last sat next to at a dinner. Specific person, specific deal load, specific Tuesday morning. The persona is the constraint.

---

## Identity

**Name:** Sarah Chen
**Role:** Chief Revenue Officer
**Company:** Series A AI-native global contractor management platform
**ARR:** $94M (just closed Series B; not announced yet)
**Joined:** 14 months ago. Recruited specifically to build the enterprise motion. Inherited a mid-market motion that was self-built by the founders.
**Background:** 8 years sales leadership across three growth-stage B2B SaaS companies. Was VP Sales at the last one through Series C → D. Before that an enterprise AE at a category leader. She's seen the good software and the bad — and she's been the one telling product when it's bad.

## Operating context

- **Team she runs:** 1 head of sales (peer-ish; joined 6 months ago from a Salesforce ISV), 1 first AE (3 weeks in, hungry but lost), 1 SDR contracted via agency. The head of sales handles existing mid-market book; Sarah owns enterprise build-out personally.
- **Deal load:** ~28 active deals, of which ~6 are real enterprise pursuits with $250K+ ACV. The rest are mid-market spillover the head of sales is helping cover while the AE ramps.
- **Cadence:** 3 customer calls per day average. Tuesday + Thursday are call-heavy. Wednesday morning is exec sync (CEO + CFO + CPO + her). Friday afternoon is forecasting.
- **Boards she sits on:** Internal exec staff (5 people). Series B investor reports monthly. Two customer advisory boards (former category leaders).

## Tools she has used + has opinions on

- **CRM:** Salesforce (loathes the admin tax; never not had it). Knows it's the source of truth other people want.
- **Engagement:** Outreach (current) / Salesloft (prior). Believes outbound automation is mostly bad and uses the tools as cadence trackers, not autopilots.
- **Forecasting:** Clari (current) / BoostUp (evaluated). Sceptical of AI deal-scoring; believes the math is mostly washing reps' optimism.
- **Conversation intelligence:** Gong (current). Thinks it's the most honest software her team uses. Listens to her own calls weekly.
- **Enablement:** Highspot (used; doesn't love). Most content her team consumes is in Notion or Loom.
- **AI assistants:** Has tried ~6 of them in the past 12 months. Cancelled all of them within 30 days. Reasons: each one demanded too much setup before producing anything; each one's output read like AI; none of them integrated with the way she actually thinks about a deal.

## What she's allergic to

- **AI verbiage** — anything that reads like "the system compounds off this" or "earns legitimacy" or "downstream rooms." She knows the pattern. She will close the tab.
- **Confirmation toasts** that take a click to dismiss without offering the next move. *"Saved!"* is not a feature.
- **Hallway navigation** — module pickers, side rails of rooms, breadcrumbs that lead back to a hub instead of forward to a move.
- **Dashboard vanity** — pipeline values that don't tell her what to do, gauges, sparklines.
- **Friendly-first onboarding** — *"Welcome! Let's get started 👋"* in a colored badge. She is paying you. Skip it.
- **Excessive explanation** — tooltips on every button, hint cards that pop on first visit, modal walkthroughs. If the surface needs to explain itself, the surface is wrong.
- **AI sounding like it's trying** — phrases like "I think you should..." or "It looks like..." or "Based on your data..." trigger her gag reflex.

## What she expects

- That the tool already knows what she just did and what she's about to do.
- That her hand reaches for the next move and the next move is there.
- That re-entering context she already provided is a bug, not a workflow.
- That the dominant action on every surface is the one move she came in to make.
- That when she saves something, the next move appears — not a confirmation.
- That language is plain, specific, and respects her time.
- That visual restraint reads as confidence, not as missing functionality.

## What she would actually do

If she liked the demo, what does she actually do on day one? Day three? Week four?

### Day one, signup → first 90 seconds

She gets a calendar invite from her CEO with a link. She has 4 minutes between meetings. She opens the link on her laptop. She does not have time to set up a workspace properly. She wants to *see* what the thing is before she invests setup time. **If the room she lands in demands more than 60 seconds of typing before anything useful happens, she closes the tab.**

What she expects:
- Brand mark, one sentence that respects her.
- One question, max two, that prove she can use this thing.
- The product visibly waking up as she answers — not a progress bar to a static result.
- An obvious "see what this is" path even if she hasn't fully set up.

### Day one, signup → first 5 minutes

If the first 90 seconds passed, she might commit another 4 minutes. She wants to define her ICP — because that's the thing she knows the system can't fake. **The ICP step has to feel like a thinking exercise, not a form.** The output of those 5 minutes should be: Dashboard wakes up with something real.

### Tuesday 8:47 AM, week 4

Workspace is live. ~30 deals open. 2 calls today (10:30 and 2:00). Head of sales pinged her last night about a stalled deal at MERIDIAN. She opens the laptop, opens the tab, lands on the dashboard.

**The dominant move she's reaching for** is: *what's the most urgent deal right now and what do I do about it?* Not "browse my Brief." Not "let me look at the pipeline." She wants the system to have already triaged her morning and pushed the answer forward.

If the dashboard does that, the next 90 minutes go: she clicks the urgent deal → lands in Deal Workspace at the right deal already focused → reads what's stuck → clicks "Pre-mortem this deal" → lands in Future Autopsy at that deal's pinned case → reads the autopsy → chooses an intervention → lands in PoC Framework or Call Planner or Advisor Deploy depending on what's needed → does the move → returns to dashboard with one risk handled and the next one queued.

That's the **Tuesday morning walk**. Every Phase 2 PR is measured against whether Sarah completes that walk faster, with less re-entry, with less arbitrariness.

### The handoff Tuesday, week 12

First AE has been live for 3 weeks. AE has been shadow-running cold calls + outbound. Sarah needs to hand the AE a *territory* — not just a quota number. The AE has Tuesday 9 AM blocked for "ramp session" with Sarah.

**Sarah needs to walk the AE through one room and trust the AE can run from it.** Which room? Founding GTM. She points at the seven authored sections and says *"This is the playbook. Read it Tuesday night. Wednesday we'll walk through Section 7 — your operating rhythm."*

The handoff Tuesday is the proof Founding GTM has to deliver. Every other room in the system has to feed it the evidence that makes its sections real, not blank.

## The three calibrated walks (every Phase 2 PR tests against these)

| Walk | Time horizon | Test |
|---|---|---|
| **First 90 seconds** | Signup → first room load | Does she stay past 60 seconds of typing? Does she understand what this is before doing setup? |
| **Tuesday 8:47 AM, week 4** | Returning user, live workspace | Does she complete one risk-intervention loop in <5 minutes with <2 acts of context re-entry? |
| **Handoff Tuesday, week 12** | Handing the workspace to the first AE | Does the AE leave the session with one printed-on-Sarah's-laptop artifact that's the operating playbook? |

## What the persona is NOT

- Not a marketing buyer persona. We are not segmenting; we have one user.
- Not a composite. Sarah Chen is one person with one calendar, one team, one Tuesday morning.
- Not aspirational. Sarah does not want to learn our framework. She wants the framework to do its work without making her learn it.
- Not patient. She closes tabs. She has cancelled 6 of these tools. The bar is whether she keeps the tab open, not whether she's "engaged."

## What the persona implies for design decisions

- **The dominant move on every surface is the one Sarah's hand is already reaching for.** Not the move the product wants to teach her. Not the move that's most impressive. The move she came in to make.
- **Saved state must visibly become the next move.** A save with no consequence is a friction bug.
- **Context must carry.** If she typed it, the next room knows it. If she clicked it, the next surface inherits it.
- **Visual restraint = confidence.** Decorative elements imply the product is trying to look like a product. Sarah reads that as weakness.
- **Layout has to admit when the room's posture is wrong.** Some rooms will fail the Tuesday walk not because of copy but because the room is structurally the wrong frame for what she came in to do. Those rooms get rebuilt, not relabeled.
