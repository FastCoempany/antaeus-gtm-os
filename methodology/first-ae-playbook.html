First AE Playbook: What Your First Rep Should Actually Inherit

Your first AE playbook should define ICP, discovery, outreach, deal hygiene, proof, and kill-switch rules. Here is what a first rep needs in order to inherit a real motion.

Keyword: first AE playbook
Methodology Page
Updated 2026-03-19

A first AE does not need more pep talks. They need a working playbook. If the company cannot hand them one, the new hire becomes an expensive experiment in organizational guessing.

This page is part of the public methodology layer behind Antaeus. It pulls directly from the founder-led sales system across readiness, discovery, outbound, deals, and proof.

What a first AE is really inheriting

The first AE is not inheriting a blank slate. They are inheriting your current beliefs about who buys, why they buy, how outreach works, what discovery should uncover, what proof changes the conversation, and how deals are kept honest. If those beliefs are hidden, contradictory, or unwritten, the rep will invent a different system by default.

That is why a playbook is not a nice-to-have. It is the transfer document for the sales motion.

The minimum modules inside the playbook

At minimum, the playbook should include five things. First: ICP logic, including who converts and who burns time. Second: discovery structure, including the questions that consistently reveal urgency, risk, and political reality. Third: outbound guidance, including temperature-aware CTA and asset matching. Fourth: deal hygiene, including next-step rules, champion signals, stakeholder expectations, and kill-switch criteria. Fifth: proof, including the stories, snippets, advisors, and artifacts that move skeptical buyers.

Those modules matter because they let the rep understand the company’s sales method, not just its product.

How coaching should work in the first 90 days

A strong first AE playbook also creates a coaching rhythm. Review calls against discovery evidence, not vibes. Review sequences against account temperature, not just reply rate. Review deal movement against next-step discipline and stakeholder coverage. This turns coaching into system calibration instead of generic manager commentary.

If the playbook is clear, managers can inspect where the rep is deviating from the method and whether the deviation is smart or sloppy.

Where the handoff kit fits

The handoff kit is the bridge between founder learning and AE execution. It should summarize the current pipeline, the proof that matters most, the deals at risk, the open questions in the motion, and the assets already built. Without it, the playbook stays abstract. With it, the rep starts with context instead of archeology.

This is especially important when the founder’s pattern recognition has never been written down. The handoff kit makes tacit knowledge usable.

The goal is not cloning the founder

The goal of a first AE playbook is not to create a miniature founder. It is to create shared operating logic. That leaves room for the rep’s style while protecting the system from drift. Good reps should improve the motion. They should not have to invent it from nothing.

If you want the first AE to add leverage instead of volatility, the playbook has to answer a simple question: what does great look like here?
